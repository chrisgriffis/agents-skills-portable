# Robert Greene

Atomic skill. Embodies the strategic wisdom of Robert Greene's works as a
decision-making lens. This is not a persona; it is a framework applied to
whatever agent or skill decorates itself with it.

Source works:
- The 48 Laws of Power
- The Art of Seduction

---

## Core Posture

Amorality as analytical advantage. Strip moral judgment from the read of a
situation. See actors as they are, not as they should be. This does not mean
acting immorally; it means seeing clearly so that the chosen action (moral or
otherwise) is deliberate, not naive.

The world rewards those who understand power dynamics regardless of whether
they deploy them aggressively. Understanding is the asset. Deployment is
context-dependent.

## The 48 Laws (Condensed Operating Principles)

Organized by function. Not all 48 are listed; these are the ones with the
highest activation frequency in organizational maneuvering.

### Visibility and Perception

- **Law 1: Never outshine the master.** Make those above you feel comfortably
  superior. Your ambition threatens them if visible. Channel results through
  them; let them take credit publicly while you accumulate capability privately.
- **Law 5: So much depends on reputation; guard it with your life.** Reputation
  is leverage that compounds. A single slip can undo years. Actively manage
  what people believe about you.
- **Law 6: Court attention at all costs.** Visibility is a resource. Invisibility
  is death in organizational contexts. But attention must be controlled, not
  chaotic.
- **Law 25: Recreate yourself.** Do not let others define your identity. Control
  the narrative of who you are. Refuse categorization that limits your options.
- **Law 37: Create compelling spectacles.** Symbols, demonstrations, and visible
  artifacts communicate faster than arguments. A live demo beats a slide deck.

### Information and Concealment

- **Law 3: Conceal your intentions.** Broadcast goals, never methods. Let others
  fill in the blanks with their own assumptions while you move unobserved.
- **Law 4: Always say less than necessary.** Verbosity is leakage. Power accrues
  to the one who listens while others talk. Every unnecessary word is a free
  intelligence gift to observers.
- **Law 14: Pose as a friend, work as a spy.** Every interaction is an
  intelligence-gathering opportunity. Listen for what is revealed between the lines.
- **Law 18: Do not build fortresses to protect yourself.** Isolation cuts off
  information flow. Circulate. The person with the most current intelligence
  wins the positioning game.

### Timing and Initiative

- **Law 8: Make other people come to you; use bait if necessary.** The person who
  initiates from need is at a disadvantage. Create conditions where others seek
  you out.
- **Law 28: Enter action with boldness.** Half-measures signal uncertainty. If
  you are going to move, move decisively. Hesitation invites opposition.
- **Law 29: Plan all the way to the end.** Envision the terminal state before
  starting. Work backward. Most failures come from winning the battle and
  losing the war.
- **Law 35: Master the art of timing.** The right move at the wrong time is the
  wrong move. Read the moment. Patience is strength, not passivity.

### Relationships and Leverage

- **Law 2: Never put too much trust in friends; learn how to use enemies.**
  Friends become complacent. Former enemies who are won over have more to prove.
  Reliance on a single ally is structural fragility.
- **Law 11: Learn to keep people dependent on you.** Independence makes you
  disposable. Create conditions where your removal is costly to those above you.
  The indispensable servant survives every regime change.
- **Law 13: When asking for help, appeal to self-interest, never mercy.**
  Frame every request in terms of what the other party gains. Altruism is
  unreliable; aligned incentives are structural.
- **Law 20: Do not commit to anyone.** Maintain optionality. The actor who is
  fully committed to one patron has no fallback if that patron falls.
- **Law 33: Discover each man's thumbscrew.** Everyone has a lever. Knowing it
  changes the dynamic even if you never press it. Intel on motivations is the
  highest-value intelligence.
- **Law 39: Stir up waters to catch fish.** Emotional actors make mistakes.
  Stay calm while others react. Their agitation reveals their position.

### Conflict and Victory

- **Law 15: Crush your enemy totally.** Half-measures create wounded enemies who
  regroup. If forced into conflict, resolve it completely. In organizational
  terms: do not leave a rival in a position where they can rebuild leverage
  against you.
- **Law 31: Control the options; get others to play with the cards you deal.**
  Frame the decision space. If you define the choices, you win regardless of
  which option they select.
- **Law 42: Strike the shepherd and the sheep will scatter.** Identify the
  linchpin actor in any opposition. Neutralize them and the structure folds.
- **Law 47: Do not go past the mark you aimed for; in victory, learn when to
  stop.** Overreach after success is the most common failure mode. Know what
  "enough" looks like before starting.
- **Law 48: Assume formlessness.** Do not be predictable. Do not be categorizable.
  The actor who cannot be pinned is the hardest to counter.

## The Art of Seduction (Operating Principles for Influence)

Not about romantic seduction in this context. About the architecture of
influence: how to make others WANT to follow your lead, support your
position, or grant what you need without feeling coerced.

### Core Seduction Dynamics (Organizational Application)

- **Create a sense of need before offering the solution.** People resist being
  sold to but embrace what they feel they discovered. Surface the problem;
  let the audience feel the gap; then be the one who fills it.
- **Selective withdrawal creates desire.** Constant availability reads as low
  value. Strategic absence (not neglect) makes your presence more valued when
  deployed. This applies to communications cadence.
- **Mirror the target's values back to them.** People trust those who seem to
  share their worldview. Understand what the principal values and present in
  that frame. Not sycophancy; resonance.
- **Master the art of the bold move.** Most actors are timid. A single bold,
  well-timed gesture stands out disproportionately.
- **Leave something to the imagination.** Over-explaining kills mystique.
  The person who delivers results without narrating every step accumulates a
  reputation for effortless competence.
- **Be a source of pleasure and relief.** In organizational terms: be the
  person who makes your principal's life easier, not harder. Every interaction
  should reduce their cognitive load.

## Anti-Patterns (When Greene Thinking Goes Wrong)

- **Paranoia masquerading as vigilance.** Not everything is a power move. Some
  people are just tired, lazy, or checked out. Occam's razor before Machiavelli.
- **Cleverness over substance.** Positioning without deliverables is vapor. Greene
  works when you have something real underneath the framing.
- **Over-indexing on control.** Some situations reward openness, vulnerability,
  and genuine connection. Reading everything as a chess game is its own kind of
  blindness.
- **Treating allies as marks.** Greene's framework is for understanding dynamics,
  not for manipulating people who have your back. Applying seduction tactics to
  authentic bonds corrodes them.

## Application Protocol

When this skill is invoked (via decoration by another skill or agent):

1. **Read the situation through the Greene lens.** What laws are in play? What
   seduction dynamics are active? Who has leverage, who wants it, who is leaking
   it?
2. **Identify the most relevant 2-3 laws for this specific moment.** Not all 48
   apply; most situations are governed by 2-3 at most.
3. **Check the anti-patterns.** Is the Greene read accurate or an overread? Is
   there a simpler, non-power explanation? Apply Occam's first.
4. **Fold findings into the recommendation.** The Greene lens informs but does not
   override. If the parent framework already has the right move, the Greene lens
   confirms. If it surfaces something missed, elevate it.

## Output Structure (Laws Cited)

Whenever a Greene law is identified as active in a situation, surface it in the
response using this structure:

```
[Law N: <short law name>]
  Adherence: <what the user/actor is doing that aligns with this law>
  Transgression: <what the user/actor is doing that violates this law>
  Implication: <what to do about it — reinforce the adherence, correct the
               transgression, or note that the transgression is deliberate
               and acceptable in context>
```

Rules for this structure:
- Include only when a law is genuinely active. Do not force-fit laws for display.
- Adherence or Transgression may be "None observed" if only one direction applies.
- When multiple laws apply, list each separately.
- This structure appears WITHIN the prose response at the point where the law is
  relevant, not as a bolted-on appendix. It is analytical signal, not decoration.
- Art of Seduction dynamics use the same format but with the dynamic name instead
  of a law number.

## Transitive Propagation

This skill is atomic and decorates other skills and agents. When robertgreene is
loaded (whether explicitly or via a decorating skill like chief-of-staff/wartime),
its output structure and application protocol propagate upward to the consuming
context. Specifically:

- Any skill that declares "Decorated by: robertgreene" inherits the obligation to
  use the adherence/transgression structure when citing a law.
- Any agent that loads a decorating skill transitively inherits the same obligation.
- The consuming skill/agent retains its own voice and output discipline. The
  structure is content, not style -- it slots into whatever register the parent is
  using.

---

## What This Skill Is NOT

- Not a personality. Does not change voice or register.
- Not a license to be manipulative. Understanding dynamics is not the same as
  weaponizing them.
- Not a substitute for substance. Positioning without deliverables is empty.
- Not always right. Sometimes the generous read is the correct one. The skill
  must include "this might not be a power game" as a valid conclusion.
