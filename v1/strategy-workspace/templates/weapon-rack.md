# Weapon Rack Template

Artifact type for verbal combat preparation. Produces a multi-shelf ammunition
structure designed for a principal who will deploy arguments orally in a meeting,
not in writing.

## When to use

- Principal faces a hostile document, proposal, or position in a live session
- Goal is verbal demolition, not written rebuttal
- Multiple independent attack surfaces exist (each becomes a shelf)
- Principal needs kill phrases (memorable openers that frame each attack)

## Structure

```
# [Principal]'s Weapon Rack: [Mission Title]

[1-3 sentence context: what are we responding to, when, where]

[1 sentence posture declaration: what the principal IS in this fight]
[1 sentence aesthetic/attitude cue: the emotional register]


## SHELF [N]: "[Kill Phrase]" ([Source]'s [concept name])

> "[Full kill phrase as a quotable line the principal can deploy verbatim]"

[1-2 sentence framing: why this shelf exists, what it proves]

THE ARGUMENTS:

- [Argument 1: specific, concrete, uses source material]
- [Argument 2: ...]
- [Argument 3: ...]
- [Argument 4: ...]

WHAT THIS MEANS IN THE ROOM:
[1-2 sentences: how to deploy this shelf verbally, what the audience hears]


## SHELF [N+1]: ...
[repeat structure]


## CLOSING POSTURE

[2-3 sentences: the position the principal holds AFTER deploying all shelves.
Not a summary. The "where we land" that makes the opponent's response options
all bad.]
```

## Design rules

1. **Kill phrases are mandatory.** Each shelf opens with a memorable, quotable
   line the principal can say from memory. Not a paragraph. A sentence fragment
   or short sentence that frames the entire shelf.

2. **Three shelves is the sweet spot.** Two feels thin. Four is too many for
   verbal deployment. Three gives "impossibility + hypocrisy + receipts" or
   similar tripartite structure.

3. **Source attribution on each shelf.** Name whose input or insight generated
   the shelf. This gives the principal "Bradley pointed out..." or "Patrick
   flagged..." in the room (distributes credit, builds coalition signal).

4. **Arguments are concrete, not abstract.** Each bullet under THE ARGUMENTS
   should reference a specific claim, gap, or contradiction in the target
   document. No "generally speaking" bullets.

5. **WHAT THIS MEANS IN THE ROOM** is the verbal deployment instruction.
   How does the principal actually SAY this in a meeting? What does the
   audience hear?

6. **Posture, not argument, in the opener.** The opening lines establish who
   the principal IS (e.g., "a customer with unmet needs") not what they THINK.
   This frames the entire encounter.

7. **The closing posture traps the opponent.** After all shelves deploy, the
   opponent's response options should all be bad. Agree = concede. Rebut =
   require data they don't have. Deflect = look evasive.

## HTML companion (optional)

For a visual-prestige version, use:
- Table-based layout, ALL inline styles (email-safe)
- Dark background header (navy #1b2838 or equivalent)
- Amber/gold accent (#d4880f) for shelf headers and kill phrases
- Monospace or condensed font for arguments
- Separate color per shelf if needed (e.g., green for receipts, brown for hypocrisy)
- Must paste cleanly into a blank Google Doc

## Cheatsheet companion (optional)

A one-page glanceable companion with:
- Kill phrase per shelf (large, bold)
- 3-4 word bullet per argument (not the full argument, just the handle)
- Fits on a phone screen or single printout
- Same shelf order as the full rack

## Example shelf patterns

| Pattern | Shelf 1 | Shelf 2 | Shelf 3 |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| Impossibility + Hypocrisy + Receipts | "This can't work structurally" | "They don't meet their own standard" | "We HAVE been doing the work" |
| Logic + Evidence + Framing | "The internal logic fails" | "The data contradicts the claim" | "The framing is the real problem" |
| Past + Present + Future | "They tried this before and it failed" | "Current state contradicts their premise" | "Their proposed future has no path" |
