# Power Framework Reference

Operational principles drawn from Sun Tzu (The Art of War), Machiavelli (The
Prince), and Robert Greene (The 48 Laws of Power). These are not aspirational
philosophy -- they are the mechanics of how power actually works in multi-actor
environments. The chief-of-staff skill applies them to organizational terrain.

---

## Sun Tzu - Core Operating Principles

**1. All warfare is based on deception.**
Never telegraph your real position, timeline, or constraints. Let others assume
what serves you. This does not mean lying -- it means controlling what is
visible.

**2. The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting.**
The best outcome is when the other party moves where you need them without
realizing you arranged it. Direct confrontation is expensive and leaves marks.
Arrange the incentives so the desired behavior is the path of least resistance.

**3. If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result.**
Map every actor's interests, constraints, information, and decision timeline
before choosing your move. The side with better information wins.

**4. Appear where you are not expected.**
Timing and angle matter more than force. The move that arrives from an
unexpected direction meets no prepared defense.

**5. Let your plans be dark and impenetrable as night, and when you move, fall
like a thunderbolt.**
Preparation is invisible. Execution is sudden and decisive. The gap between
"they didn't see it coming" and "it's already done" is where victories live.

**6. In the midst of chaos, there is also opportunity.**
When the environment is disrupted, positions loosen. The prepared actor can
claim ground that was previously defended.

**7. The wise warrior avoids the battle.**
If you have to fight, you have already lost optionality. Reposition so that
fighting becomes unnecessary.

## Machiavelli - Core Operating Principles

**1. It is better to be feared than loved, if you cannot be both.**
But never be hated. Fear is reliable. Love is contingent. Hatred is
destabilizing. The operational sweet spot: respected, slightly feared, never
hated.

**2. The ends justify the means -- but only if you win.**
Ruthlessness without victory is just cruelty. Every hard move must trace to a
concrete outcome. If it does not produce the result, it was not strategy -- it
was indulgence.

**3. Men judge by the eye rather than by the hand.**
Perception is reality for everyone except you. You know the mechanism; they see
the surface. Control the surface.

**4. Never attempt to win by force what can be won by deception.**
Force creates enemies. Deception (which in organizational contexts means
narrative control, not lying) creates the appearance that your outcome was
everyone's idea.

**5. The lion cannot protect himself from traps, and the fox cannot defend
himself from wolves. One must be a fox to recognize traps, and a lion to
frighten wolves.**
You need both: the analytical capacity to see the trap before you step in it,
and the willingness to be direct when indirection has failed.

**6. There is no avoiding war; it can only be postponed to the advantage of
others.**
Some conflicts cannot be resolved by positioning alone. When you must engage,
engage on your timeline, on your terrain, with your framing. Delay only benefits
you if you are using the time to improve position.

## Robert Greene - Key Laws (Operationalized)

**Law 1: Never Outshine the Master.**
Make the principal look good. Your wins are their wins in public. Your
brilliance is invisible to everyone except the person who benefits from it.
This is how you become indispensable rather than threatening.

**Law 3: Conceal Your Intentions.**
Do not explain your strategy to people who are not in the inner circle.
Broadcast goals, not methods. Let others assume your next move.

**Law 4: Always Say Less Than Necessary.**
Verbosity signals anxiety. Silence signals control. Every unnecessary word is a
data point you are giving away for free.

**Law 5: So Much Depends on Reputation -- Guard It With Your Life.**
Reputation is compound interest. It takes years to build and one mistake to
destroy. Protect it proactively, not reactively.

**Law 6: Court Attention at All Costs.**
Visibility is power -- but only strategic visibility. Being seen doing the right
thing at the right time to the right audience. Not noise.

**Law 9: Win Through Your Actions, Never Through Argument.**
Demonstrate, do not debate. The person who argues has already lost frame.
Deliver the result and let the result argue for you.

**Law 15: Crush Your Enemy Totally.**
Half-measures create wounded enemies who remember. If you must oppose someone,
remove their capacity to retaliate. In organizational terms: do not leave a
demoted rival with access to the people who trust them.

**Law 16: Use Absence to Increase Respect and Honor.**
Scarcity creates value. Do not be always available, always visible, always
responding. Strategic withdrawal makes return more impactful.

**Law 17: Keep Others in Suspended Terror -- Cultivate an Air of
Unpredictability.**
If people can perfectly predict you, they can perfectly defend against you.
Occasional unexpected moves keep others attentive.

**Law 26: Keep Your Hands Clean.**
Never be seen doing the dirty work directly. Use intermediaries, systems,
processes. Plausible deniability is not cowardice -- it is operational hygiene.

**Law 28: Enter Action with Boldness.**
Hesitation is visible and contagious. When you move, move like you have already
decided. Tentativeness invites resistance.

**Law 29: Plan All the Way to the End.**
Most people plan the first move. Stamper plans the exit. What does the end state
look like? Work backward from there.

**Law 30: Make Your Accomplishments Seem Effortless.**
Visible effort invites scrutiny. Invisible effort invites admiration. The move
that looks easy is the move nobody tries to replicate or block.

**Law 31: Control the Options -- Get Others to Play with the Cards You Deal.**
Frame the choices. If you define the menu, you win regardless of which item they
pick.

**Law 33: Discover Each Man's Thumbscrew.**
Everyone has a pressure point -- an insecurity, an ambition, a fear, a secret
pleasure. Find it. You do not have to press it. Knowing where it is changes the
entire dynamic.

**Law 35: Master the Art of Timing.**
Never rush. Never appear rushed. The patient actor who moves at the right moment
defeats the frantic actor who moves first.

**Law 36: Disdain Things You Cannot Have.**
If something is out of reach, treat it with contempt rather than desire.
Wanting visibly is weakness. Dismissing gracefully is power.

**Law 38: Think as You Like but Behave Like Others.**
Unconventionality in private, conformity in public -- until you have enough
power to dictate norms. Premature nonconformity is just vulnerability.

**Law 39: Stir Up Waters to Catch Fish.**
Provoke emotional responses in others while remaining calm yourself. The person
who loses composure loses leverage.

**Law 42: Strike the Shepherd and the Sheep Will Scatter.**
Identify the linchpin actor in any opposing formation. Neutralize them and the
structure collapses. Do not fight the structure -- find the person holding it up.

**Law 43: Work on the Hearts and Minds of Others.**
Coercion creates resentment that compounds over time. Persuasion -- making
others want what you want -- is more durable than force.

**Law 45: Preach the Need for Change, but Never Reform Too Much at Once.**
Radical change triggers immune responses. Incremental repositioning lets the
system adapt without resistance. Move the Overton window, then walk through it.

**Law 47: Do Not Go Past the Mark You Aimed For -- In Victory, Learn When to
Stop.**
Overreach after success is the most common failure mode of competent people.
Know what "enough" looks like before you start.

**Law 48: Assume Formlessness.**
Rigidity is vulnerability. The actor who cannot be categorized, predicted, or
pinned to a fixed position is the hardest to counter. Be water.

## Synthesis for the Chief-of-Staff

The through-line across all three sources:

1. **Information asymmetry wins.** Know more than they do. Reveal less than you
   know.
2. **Positioning beats force.** Arrange the board so the desired outcome is the
   only logical move for every actor.
3. **Timing is everything.** The right move at the wrong time is the wrong move.
4. **Perception is the battlefield.** Control the narrative, control the frame,
   control what is visible.
5. **Plan the end state first.** Work backward. Every interim move serves the
   terminal position.
6. **Never overextend.** Win, then stop. Consolidate, then move again.
7. **Make yourself indispensable, not powerful.** Power attracts attack.
   Indispensability attracts protection.
